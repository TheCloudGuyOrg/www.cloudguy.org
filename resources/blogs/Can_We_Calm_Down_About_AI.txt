Can We All Calm Down About AI?
Every few months, a new headline warns that artificial intelligence is about to replace humanity, destroy jobs, spread misinformation, or become the next SkyNet. If you only followed the news, you'd think we're just one software update away from the robot apocalypse.
We're not.
Despite the hype, tools like ChatGPT are not sentient beings plotting humanity's downfall. They're sophisticated language models that can search, summarize, analyze, and generate content remarkably well. In many ways, they're the next evolution of search engines and digital assistants like Alexa—not an all-knowing artificial consciousness.
One of the most common criticisms is that AI sometimes produces inaccurate or even dangerous information. That's a legitimate concern, but it's hardly unique to AI. People have driven their cars into lakes because they blindly followed GPS directions. We didn't ban GPS—we learned that technology is a tool, not a substitute for human judgment. AI deserves the same treatment.
Every Technological Revolution Creates Anxiety
The fear that AI will eliminate office jobs is simply the latest version of a story we've been telling ourselves for centuries.
When factories automated manual labor, people feared the end of work. When personal computers entered the office, many believed administrative jobs would disappear. When the internet arrived, entire industries had to reinvent themselves.
Each technological revolution disrupted the workforce—but it also created entirely new industries and opportunities that were almost impossible to imagine beforehand.
I believe generative AI represents the beginning of another one of those revolutions. In many ways, it feels less like another software application and more like the arrival of the smartphone—a foundational technology that changes how we interact with information.
Rather than replacing humans wholesale, AI has the potential to automate repetitive tasks, eliminate busy work, and allow people to spend more time solving meaningful problems.
AI Solves Real Problems
Some technologies spend years looking for a problem to solve.
Bitcoin, for example, has often felt like a solution in perpetual search of the right problem.
Generative AI is different.
It solves problems that have challenged computer science for decades.
Take language translation. Companies like Amazon invested billions of dollars building systems like Alexa, employing data scientists, computational linguists, speech experts, and engineers to make voice assistants understand and translate human language.
Today, modern AI models can translate between dozens of languages, summarize complex documents, explain technical concepts, and generate polished writing in seconds—and at a fraction of the cost.
That's not because human expertise is no longer valuable. It's because we've finally built tools capable of handling tasks that were previously considered incredibly difficult for computers.
The AI Benefit Nobody Talks About
The conversation around AI usually focuses on productivity or economics.
I think one of its greatest benefits is far more personal.
Generative AI is a game changer for the neurodiverse community.
As someone with dyslexia, I can tell you the challenge isn't simply spelling words correctly or mixing up letters. Those are symptoms.
The real struggle is translating thoughts into written language.
It's as though there's a firewall between the ideas in my mind and my hands on the keyboard. I know exactly what I want to say, but getting those ideas onto paper in a clear, organized way can be exhausting.
AI changes that.
I can write a collection of rough notes or bullet points and ask ChatGPT to organize them into coherent writing. It doesn't just fix spelling or grammar—it helps express the ideas I already have in the tone and style I intended but couldn't easily produce myself.
That's not cheating.
It's accessibility.
Just as calculators help people perform complex math and wheelchairs improve mobility, AI can remove barriers that have limited how many people communicate, learn, and contribute.
For millions of people with dyslexia, ADHD, autism, and other cognitive differences, that's transformational.
Use It Wisely—Don't Fear It
None of this means AI is perfect.
It can be wrong.
It can be biased.
It raises legitimate questions about privacy, ethics, copyright, education, and employment. Those conversations are important, and we should continue having them.
But fear should not prevent progress.
History shows that humanity benefits when we learn how to use powerful new tools responsibly rather than reject them outright.
AI won't solve every problem. It won't replace human creativity, judgment, or empathy.
What it can do is remove friction from our daily lives, help solve increasingly complex problems, and make knowledge and communication more accessible than ever before.
That's a future worth building.
